**Random Sampling Techniques**

Random sampling, also known as **probability sampling** or **chance sampling**, is a method of selecting a sample where every item or person in the population has an **equal or known chance of inclusion** in the sample.

This method ensures the **Law of Statistical Regularity**, which suggests that if the sample chosen is random, it will have the same composition and characteristics as the universe. The results obtained from probability sampling can be assessed in terms of probability, making it possible to measure errors of estimation or the significance of the results, which highlights its superiority over deliberate (non-random) sampling designs.

In random sampling, the selection process is mechanical, determined by **blind chance** rather than deliberation, and successive selections are independent of one another.

### Types of Random Sampling Techniques

Probability sampling techniques are generally categorized into unrestricted sampling (simple random sampling) and restricted sampling (complex random sampling).

#### 1. Simple Random Sampling (SRS)

Simple Random Sampling is the foundational probability technique where each and every item in the finite population has an **equal chance of inclusion** in the sample, and each possible sample combination has the same probability of being selected.

**Key Characteristics and Methods:**

*   **Equal Probability:** It gives every element in the population an equal probability of getting into the sample, and all choices are independent.
*   **Definition:** A simple random sample from a finite population is chosen such that each of the possible samples has the same probability of being selected.
*   **Methods of Selection:** SRS can be performed using:
    *   **Lottery Method:** Writing the names or numbers of all population elements on slips of paper, mixing them thoroughly, and drawing the required number of slips (without looking). This procedure ensures that in successive drawings, each remaining element has the same chance of selection.
    *   **Mechanical Randomization or Random Number Table:** Assigning numbers to all items and using random number tables (like Tippett's) to select the sample.
*   **Types of SRS:**
    *   **Simple Random Sampling without Replacement (SRSWOR):** Once an item is selected for the sample, it is not returned to the population and cannot appear in the sample again.
    *   **Simple Random Sampling with Replacement (SRSWR):** The element selected for the sample is returned to the population before the next element is selected, meaning the same element could appear multiple times in the sample.

#### 2. Complex Random Sampling Designs (Restricted Probability Sampling)

Complex random sampling designs are methods used when simple random sampling is restricted or less efficient. They may also be called 'mixed sampling designs' because they often combine probability and non-probability procedures (e.g., in stratification).

##### a. Systematic Sampling
This technique involves selecting items from a list at regular, fixed intervals.

*   **Procedure:** A starting unit is chosen randomly (often using random numbers) from the list, and thereafter, every $n^{th}$ element is selected until the desired sample size is reached. For instance, if a 4% sample is desired, the first item is selected randomly from the first twenty-five, and then every $25^{th}$ item is automatically included.
*   **Advantages:** It is generally easier, less costly, and less time-consuming than simple random sampling, and it spreads the sample more evenly over the entire population.
*   **Requirement:** It is useful when the sampling frame (a complete list) is available and of considerable length.

##### b. Stratified Sampling
This technique is applied when the population is **heterogeneous** (not homogeneous) and needs to be divided into more manageable, non-overlapping sub-populations or **strata**.

*   **Procedure:** The population is divided into groups (strata) that are internally homogeneous but externally heterogeneous (different from each other). Then, sample items are selected from each stratum, usually using **simple random sampling**.
*   **Purpose:** The goal is to obtain a representative sample and produce more precise estimates because each stratum is more homogeneous than the total population.
*   **Allocation:** The number of items selected from each stratum can be determined by **proportional allocation** (sample size proportional to the stratum size) or **disproportionate allocation** (taking larger samples from more variable strata).
*   **Stratified Random Sampling:** The procedure of stratification followed by simple random sampling is known as stratified random sampling.

##### c. Cluster Sampling
Cluster sampling involves dividing the population into groups called **clusters** and then **randomly selecting entire groups** (clusters) rather than individual elements for inclusion in the sample.

*   **Characteristics of Clusters:** Clusters are made so that the characteristics **within the cluster are heterogeneous**, but the characteristics **between the clusters are homogeneous**.
*   **Advantage:** This method reduces cost by concentrating surveys in selected clusters and increasing the efficiency of fieldwork. Estimates based on cluster samples are usually more reliable per unit cost.
*   **Disadvantage:** Cluster sampling is generally **less precise** than simple random sampling and requires a larger sample size to achieve the same level of accuracy.

##### d. Area Sampling
Area sampling is highly similar to cluster sampling, specifically when the clusters are defined as **geographic subdivisions**.

*   **Definition:** Under area sampling, the total geographical area of interest is divided into smaller non-overlapping areas (geographical clusters), a number of these smaller areas are randomly selected, and **all units** in these small areas are included in the sample.
*   **Utility:** It is particularly helpful when a list of the population is not available.

##### e. Multi-stage Sampling
This is a further development of cluster sampling, typically used for **big inquiries** extending over a large geographical area.

*   **Procedure:** Sampling is carried out in stages. For example, sampling banks might involve selecting states first, then districts within states, then towns within districts, and finally, sampling specific families or banks within the chosen towns.
*   **Multi-stage Random Sampling:** If the technique of random sampling is applied at all stages of selection, the procedure is termed multi-stage random sampling.
*   **Advantages:** It is easier to administer because the sampling frame is developed in partial units, and a large number of units can be sampled for a given cost due to sequential clustering.

##### f. Sampling with Probability Proportional to Size (PPS)
This technique is appropriate when cluster sampling units do not have approximately the same number of elements.

*   **Procedure:** The probability of each cluster being included in the sample is proportional to the size of the cluster. This method requires listing the number of elements in each cluster and then sampling systematically from the cumulative totals.

##### g. Sequential Sampling
This is a complex sample design where the ultimate size of the sample is **not fixed in advance** but is determined sequentially based on mathematical decisions derived from information yielded as the survey progresses. This design is often adopted in acceptance sampling plans in statistical quality control.

## 1. Independent T-Test

The **t-test** is a parametric statistical test primarily used to compare the means (averages) of two groups to determine if there is a statistically significant difference between them.

### When the Independent T-Test is Used

The independent t-test (specifically for the difference between means of two independent samples) is used under the following conditions:

1.  **Nature of Data/Scale:** The test is appropriate for data measured on an **interval** or **ratio scale** (metric data), where the mean is the appropriate measure of central tendency. It compares the averages of a **numerical variable** for two categories of a categorical variable.
2.  **Number of Samples:** It is used when comparing **two independent samples** (i.e., the samples are unrelated).
3.  **Sample Size:** It is considered appropriate for judging the significance of the difference between the means of two samples in the case of **small sample(s)** ($n < 30$), especially when the **population variance is not known**.
4.  **Assumptions (Parametric Test):** Being a parametric test, it assumes that the population(s) from which the samples are drawn are approximately **normal**, and in the case of testing the difference between means, the population variances are often assumed to be **equal**.
5.  **Purpose:** The null hypothesis ($H_0$) usually states that the means of the two populations do not differ ($\mu_1 = \mu_2$).

| Key Function | Test Conditions |
| :--- | :--- |
| **Compares means** of a numerical variable. | Two **independent** samples. |
| **Small sample size** ($n < 30$). | Population variance is **unknown**. |
| Requires data to be at least **intervally scaled**. | Assumes **normal population** distribution and **equal variances**. |

## 2. Chi-Square ($\chi^2$) Test

The **Chi-Square ($\chi^2$) test** is a non-parametric statistical measure used primarily for analyzing categorical (non-metric) data.

### When the Chi-Square Test is Used

The $\chi^2$ test is appropriate in the following situations:

1.  **Nature of Data/Scale:** It is specifically used for **categorical data** (nominal or ordinal scales) based on frequencies. It compares categorical variables within the same sample group.
2.  **As a Test of Independence/Association:** The most common use is to judge the **significance of association between two attributes** (variables), where the data is organized in a cross-tabulation table (contingency table). The null hypothesis is that the two attributes are **independent** (or not associated).
3.  **As a Test of Goodness of Fit:** It is used to see how well an assumed **theoretical distribution** (e.g., Binomial or Normal) fits the **observed data**. The test assesses the actual data values to see what would be expected if the null hypothesis were valid.
4.  **Assumptions (Non-Parametric Test):** As a non-parametric test, it requires no rigid assumptions about the population distribution. However, certain conditions must be met for its application:
    *   Observations must be collected on a random basis and be independent.
    *   No group (cell frequency) should contain very few items (e.g., typically less than 10, though some suggest 5).
    *   The overall number of items must be reasonably large (normally at least 50).

| Key Function | Test Conditions |
| :--- | :--- |
| Tests **association/independence** between attributes. | Variables must be **categorical** (nominal/ordinal). |
| Tests **goodness of fit** between observed and expected frequencies. | Requires large sample size (typically $\ge 50$) and sufficient expected cell frequencies (typically $\ge 10$). |

2.  **Evaluating User Acceptance of a New Software Interface:**

### Explanation of Terms

**Referencing**
Referencing is the standard procedure for **acknowledging sources of information** that are utilized in research. It involves **creating a bibliographic description** (a reference) of each source used in an accurate and consistent way in research writings. This detailed listing is typically placed at the end of the paper. The Reference section should include the names of the authors along with the details of the publication of their work.

**Citation (Work Citation)**
Citation is the **style of managing the information** obtained from various sources within the body of the research report. It is the act of **referring from the text to the sources used**, serving to indicate that specific information came from the list presented at the end of the research writing. Citations in the text provide brief information, usually consisting of the **author's name and the year of publication**, which guides the reader to the full reference entry. APA utilizes a system of brief referencing in the text, whether one is paraphrasing or providing a direct quotation.
