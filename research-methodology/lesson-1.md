**Research: Meaning and Definition**

Research, in common parlance, refers simply to a **search for knowledge**. It is an active, diligent, and systematic process of inquiry aimed at discovering, interpreting, or revising facts, events, behaviors, or theories, or making practical applications with the help of existing facts, laws, or theories.

More technically, research can be defined by several perspectives:

1.  **Systematic Effort to Gain New Knowledge:** Research is described as a **"systematized effort to gain new knowledge"**. It is a systematic and organized effort to investigate a specific problem or question to find answers or solutions.
2.  **Scientific Investigation:** Research is considered an art of scientific investigation. It is the search for knowledge through an **objective and systematic method of finding a solution to a problem**.
3.  **Process and Contribution:** The systematic approach involving generalization and the formulation of a theory is defined as research. Research is also viewed as an **original contribution to the existing stock of knowledge**. It is the pursuit of truth with the help of study, observation, comparison, and experiment.
4.  **Operational Steps:** Research comprises defining and redefining problems, formulating a hypothesis or suggested solutions, collecting, organizing, and evaluating data, making deductions, reaching conclusions, and carefully testing the conclusions to see if they fit the formulated hypothesis.

The word itself suggests its nature, as it is a composition of ‘re’ (again) and ‘search’ (investigation), meaning to search again, often to find something more about a phenomenon. The process of 'search and search again' to come closer and closer to the truth is known as research.

***

**Objectives of Research**

The purpose of research is to discover answers to questions through the application of scientific procedures. The main aim is to find out the **truth which is hidden and which has not been discovered as yet**.

Research objectives generally fall into the following broad groupings:

1.  **To gain familiarity with a phenomenon or to achieve new insights into it**. Studies with this objective are termed as **exploratory or formulative research studies**. Exploration involves investigating unknown areas and gaining deeper insights into a problem or topic.
2.  **To portray accurately the characteristics of a particular individual, situation or a group**. These studies are known as **descriptive research studies**, aiming to describe characteristics, behaviors, or phenomena in detail.
3.  **To determine the frequency with which something occurs or with which it is associated with something else**. These are known as **diagnostic research studies**.
4.  **To test a hypothesis of a causal relationship between variables**. Such studies are called **hypothesis-testing research studies**.

Other objectives include:

*   **Explanation:** To explain why certain things happen and understand the relationships between variables.
*   **Prediction:** To predict future events or outcomes based on current knowledge.
*   **Application:** To use research findings to solve real-world problems or improve systems.

***

**Motivation of Research**

The possible motives for undertaking research may involve one or more factors. These motivating factors include:

*   **Career Advancement and Degree:** The desire to get a research degree along with its consequential benefits.
*   **Intellectual and Creative Drive:**
    *   A natural desire to explore and understand unknown phenomena or solve unanswered questions.
    *   The desire to face the challenge in solving unsolved problems, often initiated by concern over practical problems.
    *   The desire to get the intellectual joy of doing some creative work.
    *   Curiosity about new things.
    *   The desire to understand causal relationships.
    *   The drive to expand the boundaries of what is known.
*   **Social and Practical Impact:**
    *   The desire to be of service to society.
    *   To address specific challenges or find solutions to practical issues.
    *   To contribute to societal development and progress through innovation and solutions.
    *   To assist governments or organizations in forming informed policies and decisions.
    *   Social thinking and awakening.
*   **Recognition and Incentives:**
    *   The desire to get respectability.
    *   Receiving fellowships from government and private agencies.
    *   Achieving a sense of fulfillment through contributing to knowledge or solving problems.
*   **Professional Needs:** Knowing the employment condition.

This list is not exhaustive, as factors like government directives may also compel people to perform research operations.

***

**Clarification through Analogy:**

If we consider knowledge as a vast, dark forest, **research** is the disciplined expedition into that darkness, systematically mapping the terrain, classifying unknown flora and fauna, and continually checking the path (methodology). The **objective** is the specific treasure we seek—whether it is simply describing a newly found river (descriptive research), proving the existence of a rare medicinal plant (hypothesis testing), or using the knowledge to build a useful bridge across the river (application). The **motivation** is why the explorer ventures forth—it might be for fame (respectability), gold (degree/fellowship), or the sheer thrill of discovery (intellectual joy/curiosity).

The concepts of inductive theory and deductive theory represent two fundamental approaches to conducting research and relating theory to empirical observation.

Both induction and deduction are critical components of the logical reasoning that guides systematic research.

### I. Deductive Theory (The Top-Down Approach)

Deductive theory is a research approach that begins with a broad theoretical framework or a formalized idea, and then proceeds to test this idea in a specific context.

| Aspect | Description | Citation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Starting Point** | It starts with the **proposition of a hypothesis**. | |
| **End Result** | The end result of the research is the **confirmation or rejection** of the initial hypothesis. | |
| **Core Concern** | It is concerned with **testing an existing theory**. | |
| **Logical Principle** | The main principle is reasoning from **general to specific**. It moves from **theory to example** and from **abstract to concrete**. | |
| **Logical Process** | Deduction is the process of reasoning from **some premise to a conclusion which follows from that very premise**. | |
| **Methodological Structure**| This approach is **highly structured**, as the researcher starts with the hypothesis itself and is **bound not to alter the direction of the study**. | |
| **Application** | It is appropriate for **large sample projects** because large numbers of samples are analyzed. | |
| **Conceptual Flow** | The top-down process involves moving from **Theory → Hypothesis → Pattern → Confirm/reject**. | |

***

### II. Inductive Theory (The Bottom-Up Approach)

Inductive theory is a research approach that starts with specific observations or real-world data and uses those findings to formulate broader generalizations, eventually building a theory.

| Aspect | Description | Citation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Starting Point** | It starts with **observation**. | |
| **End Result** | The end result of the research is the **theory**. | |
| **Core Concern** | It is concerned with **building a new theory**. | |
| **Logical Principle** | The main principle is reasoning from **specific to general**. It moves from **example to theory** and from **concrete to abstract**. | |
| **Logical Process** | Induction is the process of reasoning **from a part to the whole**. | |
| **Methodological Structure**| This approach is **less structured**, and the researcher is **free to alter the direction of the study** because it does not begin with a hypothesis. | |
| **Application** | It is appropriate for **small sample projects** because small numbers of samples are analyzed with **greater gravity** (more scrutiny). This approach often assumes an **abundance of time** for depth analysis. | |
| **Conceptual Flow** | The bottom-up process involves moving from **Observation → Pattern → Tentative hypothesis → Theory**. | |

***

### Comparison of Deductive and Inductive Theory

| Feature | Deductive Theory | Inductive Theory | Citation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Purpose** | Testing an existing theory. | Building a new theory. | |
| **Direction/Approach** | Top-to-bottom approach. | Bottom-to-up approach. | |
| **Start** | Proposition of hypothesis. | Observation. | |
| **Movement** | General to specific (abstract to concrete). | Specific to general (concrete to abstract). | |
| **Structure** | Highly structured. | Less structured. | |
| **Ideal Sample Size** | Large sample projects. | Small sample projects. | |

**Metaphorical Understanding:**

If acquiring knowledge is like constructing a path, **deductive theory** is using a detailed, predetermined blueprint (the existing theory) to lay the pavement, ensuring the path conforms exactly to the original design (confirming the hypothesis). Conversely, **inductive theory** is like exploring uncharted wilderness (observations), noting repeated landmarks (patterns), and then drawing a map that describes the territory you discovered for the first time (building a new theory).
