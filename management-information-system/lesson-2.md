# Business Processes & Their Relationship to Information Systems

## WHAT IS A BUSINESS PROCESS?

### Definition:
**Set of logically related tasks and behaviors** that organizations develop over time to produce specific business results in a unique manner

### Key Point:
- How activities are **organized and coordinated** = source of competitive strength
- Different from other companies' processes = competitive advantage

---

## EXAMPLES OF BUSINESS PROCESSES:

- Developing a new product
- Generating and fulfilling a customer order
- Creating a marketing plan
- Hiring an employee
- Processing a customer complaint
- Manufacturing a product
- Delivering a service

---

## HOW IS IMPROVES BUSINESS PROCESSES

### 1. **AUTOMATION** - Speed & Efficiency

**Example: Ponsse (Wood Harvesting)**
- **Old Process**: Manual cutting decisions, paper maps, verbal instructions
- **New Process**: 
  - GPS-guided harvesters
  - Automated cutting optimization
  - Real-time data transmission via email
  - Forwarders use GPS to navigate
- **Result**: Faster, more accurate, fewer errors

**Example: Walmart Supply Chain**
- **Old Process**: Manual inventory checks, slow reordering
- **New Process**: 
  - Barcode scanning at checkout
  - Real-time data to suppliers
  - Automatic reordering
- **Result**: Zero inventory waste, $28/sq ft sales (highest in industry)

---

### 2. **INFORMATION FLOW** - Better Coordination

**Example: JCPenney & TAL Apparel (Supplier Intimacy)**
- **Old Process**: 
  - JCPenney orders shirts → shipped to warehouse → distributed to stores
  - High inventory costs, slow response
- **New Process**:
  - Sale happens at register
  - Data instantly sent to TAL in Hong Kong
  - TAL computer model calculates what to produce
  - Shirts shipped directly to stores (bypassing warehouse)
- **Result**: 
  - Zero inventory at JCPenney
  - Reduced costs
  - Faster response to demand

**Example: Disney Crowd Management**
- **Input Data**: 
  - Airline bookings
  - Hotel reservations
  - Weather data
  - Historic attendance
  - Live crowd cameras
- **Process**: Computer calculates attendance projections
- **Output**: 
  - Wait times per ride
  - Which attractions overcrowded
  - Which have spare capacity
- **Result**: Better crowd management, higher customer satisfaction

---

### 3. **DECISION-MAKING** - Real-Time Information

**Example: Verizon Digital Dashboard**
- **Old Process**: Managers guessing where problems are, slow response
- **New Process**:
  - Real-time data on customer complaints
  - Network performance by location
  - Line outages/storm damage
- **Result**: 
  - Immediate resource allocation to affected areas
  - Faster service restoration
  - Better customer communication

**Example: Mandarin Oriental Hotels**
- **Old Process**: Staff manually checking guest preferences
- **New Process**:
  - Computer stores guest preferences (room temp, check-in time, TV shows, phone numbers)
  - System automatically adjusts room settings
  - Personalized marketing campaigns
- **Result**: 
  - Better customer experience
  - Increased customer loyalty
  - Higher revenues

---

### 4. **INTEGRATION** - Connecting Different Functions

**Example: Sunbelt Rentals Mobile SalesPro App**
- **Connects**:
  - Point-of-sale system (what's selling)
  - Inventory system (what's available)
  - Enterprise system (all business data)
- **Old Process**: Sales team had to check multiple systems, slow quotes
- **New Process**: 
  - Single app shows everything
  - Sales team at job site can give instant quotes
  - Based on real-time rental rates & equipment availability
- **Result**: Faster sales, better customer service

---

### 5. **OPTIMIZATION** - Better Resource Use

**Example: Ponsse Harvester Cutting Optimization**
- **Old Process**: Harvester driver manually decides where to cut logs
- **New Process**:
  - System calculates optimal cutting points
  - As log moves through cutting head, system finds best places to cut
  - Minimizes waste
- **Result**: 
  - Higher yield per tree
  - Less waste
  - More profit

**Example: UPS Package Delivery**
- **Old Process**: Drivers chose routes manually
- **New Process**:
  - DIAD device calculates optimal routes
  - Considers traffic, delivery points, time windows
- **Result**: 
  - Saves 28 million miles annually
  - Saves 3 million gallons of fuel
  - Faster deliveries

# Systems for Different Management Groups

## OVERVIEW

Organizations use different types of information systems to support different management levels. Each system serves specific needs and provides different types of information.

---

## THE THREE MANAGEMENT LEVELS & THEIR SYSTEMS

### 1. OPERATIONAL MANAGERS (Daily Operations)

**System Type: Transaction Processing Systems (TPS)**

#### What It Does:
Tracks daily business transactions like sales, receipts, cash deposits, payroll, and credit decisions.

#### Examples:
- **UPS DIAD System**: Captures package signatures, delivery confirmations, barcode scans
- **Walmart POS System**: Records every sale at checkout
- **Ponsse Harvester System**: Records cutting data, machine performance
- **Disney Ticketing**: Processes ticket sales in real-time

#### Information Provided:
- Transaction details (who, what, when, where, how much)
- Immediate operational data
- Daily activity summaries

#### Who Uses It:
- Operational/production managers
- Store managers
- Shift supervisors
- Front-line employees

#### Key Characteristic:
Focuses on **processing routine transactions** rather than analysis

---

### 2. MIDDLE MANAGERS (Tactical Level)

**System Type: Management Information Systems (MIS)**

#### What It Does:
Summarizes TPS data and provides regular reports on performance metrics. Goes beyond simply tracking activity.

#### Examples:
- **Walmart Regional Reports**: Sales by store, inventory levels, supplier performance
- **Verizon Performance Reports**: Customer complaints by region, network outages, response times
- **Disney Attendance Reports**: Daily crowd levels, wait times by attraction, revenue by park
- **Mandarin Oriental Reports**: Guest satisfaction scores, room occupancy rates, revenue per available room

#### Information Provided:
- Performance summaries and comparisons
- Trends and patterns
- Regular scheduled reports
- Departmental performance metrics

#### Who Uses It:
- Middle managers
- Department heads
- Regional managers
- Team coordinators

#### Key Characteristics:
- **Structured decisions** (following established procedures)
- **Regular reporting** (daily, weekly, monthly)
- **Less analytical** than DSS
- Helps **identify inefficiencies** and **prioritize attention**

---

### 3. SENIOR EXECUTIVES (Strategic Level)

**System Types:**
- **Decision Support Systems (DSS)**
- **Executive Information Systems (EIS)**
- **Business Intelligence Systems**

#### What They Do:
Deliver comprehensive data and tools to help senior managers make strategic decisions. Provide quick access to summarized reports from all company levels.

#### Examples:

**Verizon Digital Dashboard:**
- Real-time data on customer complaints
- Network performance by location
- Line outages and storm damage
- Allows immediate strategic resource allocation

**Disney Strategic System:**
- Airline booking data
- Hotel reservations
- Weather forecasts
- Historic attendance patterns
- Live crowd cameras
- **Output**: Attendance projections, wait time predictions, capacity planning

**Valero Refining Dashboard:**
- Drill down from executive level to refinery level
- Individual system-operator displays
- Key performance indicators on single screen
- Graphs and charts for quick analysis

**Dow Corning Roambi Visualizer:**
- Real-time sales data on iPhone
- CFO gets performance overview in 15 seconds
- Identifies financial issues immediately

#### Information Provided:
- Strategic trends and forecasts
- Comprehensive enterprise-wide data
- Long-term planning information
- Competitive analysis
- Risk assessment

#### Who Uses It:
- Chief Executive Officers (CEO)
- Chief Financial Officers (CFO)
- Chief Technology Officers (CTO)
- Senior executives
- Board members

#### Key Characteristics:
- **Unstructured/semi-structured decisions**
- **Analytical modeling** and forecasting
- **Problem-solving support**
- Helps analyze trends and **forecast future scenarios**
- Supports **high-level strategic decisions**

---

## HOW THESE SYSTEMS WORK TOGETHER

### Integration & Coordination:

```
OPERATIONAL LEVEL (TPS)
↓ (Raw transaction data)
MIDDLE MANAGEMENT (MIS)
↓ (Summarized reports)
SENIOR EXECUTIVES (DSS/EIS/BI)
↓ (Strategic insights)
BUSINESS DECISIONS & STRATEGY
```

### Key Point:
**Integrating these systems across the organization allows for improved information sharing and coordinated business processes**.

---

## COMPARISON TABLE

| **Aspect** | **TPS (Operational)** | **MIS (Middle Management)** | **DSS/EIS (Senior Management)** |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Users** | Operational managers, employees | Middle managers, supervisors | Senior executives, CEO, CFO |
| **Time Horizon** | Daily/immediate | Weekly/monthly | Long-term/strategic |
| **Data Focus** | Individual transactions | Summarized data | Comprehensive enterprise data |

# Systems for Linking the Enterprise

## OVERVIEW

Organizations need systems that connect different departments, functions, and business processes across the entire enterprise. These systems integrate data and coordinate activities company-wide.

---

## WHY LINK THE ENTERPRISE?

### Problems Without Integration:

**Isolated Systems (Silos):**
- Each department has separate systems
- Data doesn't communicate between departments
- Duplicate data entry
- Inconsistent information
- Slow decision-making
- Wasted resources

**Key Challenge:** How can a business manage all the information in different systems, maintain them cost-effectively, and ensure all systems share information so managers and employees can coordinate their work?

### Benefits of Linking Systems:

- **Real-time information flow** across departments
- **Coordinated decision-making**
- **Faster response to market changes**
- **Reduced costs** (no duplicate work)
- **Better customer service**
- **Competitive advantage**
- **Information as a vital resource** that enhances the bottom line

---

## MAIN SYSTEMS FOR LINKING THE ENTERPRISE

### 1. ENTERPRISE RESOURCE PLANNING (ERP) SYSTEMS

#### What Is It?
**Integrated software system that consolidates all business processes into one unified system** that serves the entire organization. Also known as enterprise systems.

#### How It Works:
- Single comprehensive data repository serves entire company
- All departments access same data
- Real-time updates across all functions
- Eliminates data silos
- Information previously fragmented in many different systems is now stored centrally

#### Key Functions Connected:
- **Sales & Marketing**: Customer orders, pricing
- **Manufacturing & Production**: Production scheduling, inventory
- **Finance & Accounting**: Billing, payroll, costs
- **Human Resources**: Employee data, benefits
- **Supply Chain**: Procurement, logistics
- **Inventory Management**: Stock levels, warehousing

#### How Orders Flow Through ERP:

When a customer places an order, the order data flows automatically to other parts of the company:
1. **Order transaction** triggers warehouse to pick products and schedule shipment
2. **Warehouse informs factory** to replenish depleted inventory
3. **Accounting department** is notified to send customer an invoice
4. **Customer service representatives** track order progress through every step

#### Real-World Examples:

**Procter & Gamble (P&G) - Decision Cockpits:**
- **Old System**: Separate systems for each region/function
- **New System**: Integrated ERP with "Decision Cockpits"
- **What It Shows**: 
  - Real-time production data
  - Sales by product and region
  - Inventory levels globally
  - Customer demand forecasts
- **Result**: 
  - Managers can pilot entire company from one dashboard
  - Faster decision-making
  - Better resource allocation

**Schiphol International Hub (Amsterdam Airport):**
- **Challenge**: Coordinate airlines, baggage, ground services, security
- **System**: Integrated ERP connecting all airport operations
- **What It Tracks**:
  - Flight schedules
  - Baggage routing
  - Ground vehicle movements
  - Security checkpoints
  - Passenger flows
- **Result**:
  - Smooth passenger experience
  - Efficient airport operations
  - Reduced delays

**Summit Electric (Case Study):**
- **Problem**: Multiple legacy systems, no integration
- **Solution**: Implemented new ERP system
- **Connected**: Sales, inventory, manufacturing, finance
- **Result**: Improved efficiency and decision-making

#### Benefits:
- **Unified data**: Single source of truth
- **Process standardization**: Same procedures company-wide
- **Efficiency**: Reduced redundancy
- **Better reporting**: Comprehensive enterprise view
- **Faster response**: Real-time information
- **Precise and timely decisions** about daily operations and longer-term planning

#### Challenges:
- **Expensive**: High implementation costs
- **Complex**: Requires significant organizational change
- **Time-consuming**: Long implementation periods
- **Training**: Employees need extensive training
- **Customization**: May need modifications for specific needs

# E-Business, E-Commerce, and E-Government

## OVERVIEW

Digital technologies have transformed how businesses operate, sell products, and interact with customers and governments. These three related concepts represent different levels of digital business transformation.

---

## KEY DEFINITIONS

### 1. E-BUSINESS

#### What Is It?
**The use of digital technologies and the Internet to execute and manage all business processes and relationships with customers, suppliers, and employees**.

#### Scope:
- **Broader than e-commerce**
- Includes all digital business activities
- Internal and external operations
- All business functions transformed by Internet

#### Examples of E-Business Activities:
- **Internal**: Employee training online, digital collaboration, remote work
- **External**: Customer service via chat, supplier communication, vendor management
- **Operations**: Digital supply chains, automated workflows
- **Marketing**: Social media, email campaigns, online advertising

#### Key Point:
E-business is about **transforming the entire business model** using digital technologies, not just selling online.

---

### 2. E-COMMERCE

#### What Is It?
**The buying and selling of goods and services over the Internet**. E-commerce is the part of e-business that deals with **buying and selling**.

#### Scope:
- **Narrower than e-business**
- Focused on transactions
- Buying and selling activities
- Can be B2B, B2C, C2C

#### Types of E-Commerce:

**B2B (Business-to-Business):**
- Businesses selling to other businesses
- **Example**: Ford AutoXchange B2B Marketplace
  - Suppliers bid on parts contracts
  - Reduces procurement costs
  - Faster ordering process

**B2C (Business-to-Consumer):**
- Businesses selling to individual customers
- **Example**: Amazon, eBay, Etsy
- **Example**: Groupon (daily deals)
  - Aggregates customer demand
  - Negotiates discounts with merchants
  - Customers get deals, merchants get volume

**C2C (Consumer-to-Consumer):**
- Consumers selling to consumers
- **Example**: eBay, Etsy marketplace
- **Example**: Etsy
  - Artisans sell handmade goods
  - Community and marketplace combined
  - Peer-to-peer transactions

**C2B (Consumer-to-Business):**
- Consumers selling services/products to businesses
- **Example**: Freelance platforms (Upwork, Fiverr)

#### E-Commerce Growth:
- **2012**: $362 billion in revenues
- **2016 Projection**: Over $542 billion
- **Mobile Commerce**: $30 billion in 2012, growing double digits annually
- **122 million Americans** access Internet via smartphones
- **184 million Americans** shop online
- **150 million Americans** have purchased online
- **67 million Americans** daily research products/services online

#### Real-World Examples:

**Groupon - Social and Local Commerce:**
- **Business Model**: Daily deals platform
- **How It Works**:
  - Aggregates customer demand in specific location
  - Negotiates discount with local merchant
  - Customers get 50%+ discount
  - Merchant gets volume of customers
- **Result**: 
  - Rapid growth to $1B+ valuation
  - New revenue stream for merchants
  - Customers discover new businesses

**Amazon:**
- Started as online bookstore
- Now sells everything
- Cloud storage for digital content
- Prime membership for fast delivery
- **Impact**: Changed retail forever

**iTunes/Apple:**
- Transformed music distribution
- From physical CDs to digital downloads
- Cloud-based content delivery
- Changed entire industry business model

**Zagat Restaurant Reviews:**
- **Challenge**: Pay model vs. free model
- User-generated content (crowdsourcing)
- Monetization through advertising and partnerships

#### E-Commerce Revenue Models:

**1. Direct Sales:**
- Sell products/services directly
- **Example**: Amazon selling books, electronics

**2. Subscription:**
- Recurring payments for access
- **Example**: Netflix, Spotify, Adobe Creative Cloud

**3. Advertising:**
- Free service, revenue from ads
- **Example**: Google, Facebook

**4. Freemium:**
- Free basic service, premium paid features
- **Example**: Dropbox, LinkedIn

**5. Transaction Fees:**
- Commission on each transaction
- **Example**: eBay, Etsy, PayPal

**6. Affiliate Marketing:**
- Commission for referring customers
- **Example**: Amazon Associates

#### Challenges of E-Commerce:
- **Trust and security**: Payment safety, data privacy
- **Logistics**: Shipping, returns, inventory
- **Competition**: Low barriers to entry
- **Customer acquisition**: High marketing costs
- **Technology**: Maintaining reliable systems

---

### 3. E-GOVERNMENT

#### What Is It?
**The use of Internet and digital technologies by government agencies to deliver services to citizens, businesses, and other government agencies**.

#### Scope:
- Government services online
- Citizen interactions with government
- Inter-agency communication

# Collaboration, Social Business, and Benefits

## WHAT IS COLLABORATION?

### Definition:
**Collaboration is a working practice where people or groups work together to achieve shared goals**. It involves coordinating activities, sharing information, and jointly solving problems.

### Key Elements:
- **Working together** toward common objectives
- **Sharing knowledge** and resources
- **Coordinating activities** across teams/departments
- **Joint problem-solving** and decision-making

### Types of Collaboration:

**1. Internal Collaboration:**
- Within the organization
- Between departments/teams
- Cross-functional projects
- **Example**: Marketing and Sales teams working together on campaign

**2. External Collaboration:**
- With customers, suppliers, partners
- Cross-sector partnerships
- Industry collaborations
- **Example**: JCPenney & TAL Apparel supply chain integration

---

## WHAT IS SOCIAL BUSINESS?

### Definition:
**Social business uses social media tools and technologies to enable collaboration and communication within and outside the organization**. It's about becoming more transparent and engendering a culture of collaboration, not competition.

### Key Characteristics:
- Uses **social networking platforms** (internal and external)
- Enables **real-time communication** and information sharing
- Creates **transparent** work environments
- Fosters **collaborative culture** over competitive culture
- Allows employees to be part of the conversation, both internal and external

### Social Business vs Traditional Business:

| **Traditional Business** | **Social Business** |
|---|---|
| Hierarchical communication | Open, networked communication |
| Information silos | Transparent information sharing |
| Formal channels only | Multiple communication channels |
| Top-down decisions | Collaborative decision-making |
| Competitive culture | Collaborative culture |

### Examples of Social Business Tools:
- **Internal**: Slack, Microsoft Teams, SharePoint, Yammer
- **External**: LinkedIn, Twitter, Facebook for business
- **Collaboration platforms**: Monday.com, Wrike, Igloo

---

# Building a Collaborative Culture and Business Processes

## WHAT IS A COLLABORATIVE CULTURE?

### Definition:
**A work environment where employees work together toward shared goals, with trust as the foundation, and where collaboration becomes a natural part of how work gets done**.

### Key Characteristics:
- **Transparency and open communication**
- **Shared vision and goals**
- **Trust-based relationships**
- **Collaborative workflows built into processes**
- **Employees empowered to contribute**
- **Leaders modeling collaborative behavior**

### Collaboration vs. Cooperation vs. Coordination:

| **Collaboration** | **Cooperation** | **Coordination** |
|---|---|---|
| Shared goals, striving to create something new | Broad but mandated goals, driven by directives | Narrow goals, driven by directive |
| Requires trust as foundation | Trust important but not essential | Less need for trust |
| Commitment is strong and shared | Commitment often uneven | Independent activity |

---

## SEVEN METHODS TO CREATE COLLABORATIVE CULTURE

### 1. **CREATE A CLEAR VISION**

**What It Means:**
Employees must clearly understand the vision, goals, and why collaboration matters. Mutual trust must be articulated as foundational to that vision.

**How It Works:**
- Establish clear vision and goals driving collaboration
- Articulate that mutual trust is foundational
- Help employees feel invested in working together
- Trust becomes the bedrock of collaborative cultures

**Example**: P&G Decision Cockpits - all managers see same vision of company performance in real-time

---

### 2. **CONDUCT TEAM-BUILDING EXERCISES**

**What It Means:**
Structured activities that accelerate team cohesion and collaborative culture, especially for newly assembled teams.

**Examples of Exercises:**

**Negative & Positive:**
- Pairs share negative professional experiences, then find silver linings
- Objective: Turn adverse situations into productive experiences

**Scavenger Hunt:**
- Teams complete absurd tasks on deadline
- Use humor to facilitate bonding and collaboration

**Escape Room:**
- Teams solve problems together with different abilities and perspectives
- Pair creative types with scientific types
- Demonstrates how complementary skills bring team success

---

### 3. **HOLD IN-OFFICE AND OUT-OF-OFFICE EVENTS**

**In-Office Events:**
- Industry expert panels and seminars
- Cross-departmental demonstrations of work results
- Informal discussions of industry trends
- Lower barriers to participation
- Accelerate connections between people

**Out-of-Office Events:**
- Team happy hours
- Volunteer opportunities during workday
- Collective community contribution
- Reinforce value placed on team and collaboration

---

### 4. **BE BOTH TASK- AND RELATIONSHIP-ORIENTED**

**What It Means:**
Managers must balance getting work done with building trust and relationships. Change leadership emphasis depending on team needs.

**Traditional Approach:**
- Task-oriented only: delegate and monitor

**Collaborative Approach:**
- Task-oriented: ensure work gets done
- Relationship-oriented: build trust with subordinates and between team members
- Good leaders can do both, changing on the fly

---

### 5. **MODEL COLLABORATIVE BEHAVIOR**

**What It Means:**
Leaders demonstrating desired behaviors is a powerful way to produce them. Actions speak louder than words.

**How It Works:**
- Bring team members to interdepartmental meetings
- Let them observe higher-level collaborations
- Share successful collaboration stories in writing
- When employees see leaders working alongside others, seeking input, and sharing credit, it sends powerful message
- Shows collaboration isn't just a buzzword — it's how things are done

---

### 6. **STOP THE FINGER-POINTING**

**What It Means:**
Eliminate blame and shame from team culture. Replace with collective problem-solving.

**How It Works:**
- When teams fall short, no finger-pointing
- Work together to identify improvements
- Learn from experience and grow together
- Blame undermines trust and lowers morale

**Key Point**: A team without trust is not a team; it is a loose collection of individuals.

---

### 7. **TALK ABOUT TRUST ISSUES**

**What It Means:**
Address trust problems directly through communication. Building trust hinges on shared values and honest dialogue.

**How It Works:**
- Use anonymous questionnaires to identify trust issues
- Share overall findings with group (not individual responses
