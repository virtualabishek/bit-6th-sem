# Business Value of Improved Decision Making

## Overview

Many business managers operate in an **information fog bank**, never really having the right information at the right time to make an informed decision. Instead, managers rely on forecasts, best guesses, and luck. The result is:
- **Over- or underproduction** of goods and services
- **Misallocation of resources**
- **Poor response times**
- **Higher costs**
- **Lost customers**

Information systems and technologies have made it possible for managers to use **real-time data from the marketplace** when making decisions.

## How Information Systems Improve Decision Making

### Access to Real-Time Information
- Managers can access up-to-date data instantly rather than waiting for reports
- Real-time dashboards provide current performance metrics
- Systems capture and process data as events occur
- Enables quick responses to changing conditions

### Better Data Quality and Analysis
- Information systems provide accurate, consistent data
- Advanced analytics tools identify patterns and trends
- Data visualization makes complex information easier to understand
- Reduces reliance on intuition and guesswork

### Enhanced Forecasting and Planning
- Predictive analytics forecast future outcomes based on historical data
- Scenario planning tools evaluate different options
- Risk assessment capabilities identify potential problems
- Improves resource allocation and strategic planning

## Business Value Examples

### Verizon Corporation Example
Verizon, one of the largest telecommunication companies in the United States, uses a **Web-based digital dashboard** to provide managers with precise real-time information on:
- Customer complaints
- Network performance for each locality served
- Line outages or storm-damaged lines

**Business value achieved:**
- Managers can **immediately allocate repair resources** to affected areas
- Can **inform consumers** of repair efforts
- Can **restore service fast**
- Improves customer satisfaction and reduces downtime costs

### Operational Benefits
- **Reduced costs** - Better decisions minimize waste and inefficiency
- **Faster response times** - Quick access to information enables rapid action
- **Improved resource allocation** - Deploy people and assets where most needed
- **Better coordination** - All managers work from same accurate information

### Strategic Benefits
- **Competitive advantage** - Make better decisions faster than competitors
- **Market responsiveness** - Adapt quickly to changing customer needs
- **Risk management** - Identify and address problems before they escalate
- **Innovation** - Data-driven insights reveal new opportunities

### Financial Impact
- **Higher revenues** - Better decisions about pricing, products, and markets
- **Lower costs** - Reduce waste, errors, and inefficiencies
- **Improved profitability** - Optimize operations and resource use
- **Better ROI** - Invest in the right projects and initiatives

## Key Capabilities That Drive Value

### Decision Support Systems (DSS)
- Provide analytical tools for specific decision-making problems
- Allow managers to model different scenarios
- Support "what-if" analysis
- Help evaluate alternatives

### Business Intelligence (BI)
- Consolidate data from multiple sources
- Provide comprehensive view of business performance
- Enable data mining and pattern discovery
- Support both operational and strategic decisions

### Executive Dashboards
- Display key performance indicators (KPIs) at a glance
- Provide drill-down capabilities for detailed analysis
- Alert managers to problems requiring attention
- Track progress toward goals

### Analytics and Reporting
- Generate reports automatically on schedule
- Provide ad-hoc query capabilities
- Perform complex statistical analysis
- Visualize data through charts and graphs

## Summary

The business value of improved decision making through information systems includes:

1. **Moving from guesswork to data-driven decisions** - Reduces errors and improves outcomes
2. **Real-time responsiveness** - Enables quick action when problems or opportunities arise
3. **Better resource utilization** - Optimizes use of people, assets, and capital
4. **Improved operational efficiency** - Reduces waste and streamlines processes
5. **Enhanced competitive position** - Makes better decisions faster than competitors
6. **Higher profitability** - Increases revenues while reducing costs

Information systems transform decision making from an art based on intuition and experience into a science supported by accurate, timely data and powerful analytical tools.



# Management Activities and Management Decision Making

## What is Management?

Management is the process of making decisions about the use of an organization's resources to accomplish its goals. Managers are responsible for planning, organizing, leading, and controlling organizational activities to achieve business objectives.

## Management Activities

### Planning
**Planning** involves defining organizational goals and determining the strategies and resources needed to achieve them.

**Planning activities include:**
- Setting organizational objectives and targets
- Developing strategies to reach goals
- Allocating resources (budget, personnel, equipment)
- Creating timelines and schedules
- Identifying risks and contingencies
- Forecasting future conditions and market trends

### Organizing
**Organizing** involves structuring the organization and assigning responsibilities to accomplish planned objectives.

**Organizing activities include:**
- Designing organizational structure and hierarchy
- Creating departments and teams
- Defining roles and responsibilities
- Establishing reporting relationships
- Allocating resources to different units
- Creating policies and procedures

### Leading
**Leading** involves motivating and directing employees to work toward organizational goals.

**Leading activities include:**
- Communicating vision and strategy
- Motivating employees
- Building team cohesion
- Providing feedback and coaching
- Making day-to-day operational decisions
- Resolving conflicts
- Inspiring innovation and change

### Controlling
**Controlling** involves monitoring performance and taking corrective action when necessary.

**Controlling activities include:**
- Setting performance standards
- Measuring actual performance
- Comparing actual to expected results
- Identifying variances and problems
- Taking corrective action
- Adjusting plans and strategies
- Monitoring compliance with policies

## Types of Managers

### Operational Managers
**Operational managers** (also called supervisors or first-line managers) are responsible for day-to-day operations and supervising non-managerial employees.

**Responsibilities:**
- Supervise frontline employees
- Ensure work is completed on time and to quality standards
- Handle immediate operational problems
- Implement decisions made by higher management
- Provide feedback to middle management

### Middle Managers
**Middle managers** are responsible for implementing organizational strategy and coordinating between operational and senior management.

**Responsibilities:**
- Supervise operational managers
- Develop tactical plans to implement strategy
- Allocate resources to departments
- Monitor departmental performance
- Communicate between senior and operational management
- Solve tactical problems

### Senior Managers
**Senior managers** (executives) are responsible for overall organizational strategy and performance.

**Responsibilities:**
- Set organizational vision and strategy
- Make major resource allocation decisions
- Represent organization to external stakeholders
- Monitor overall organizational performance
- Make long-term strategic decisions
- Ensure organizational survival and growth

## Management Decision Making

### Types of Decisions

**Structured Decisions**
- **Structured decisions** are repetitive and routine, with clear procedures for handling them.
- Have well-defined problems with standard solutions
- Can be programmed into information systems
- Examples: inventory reordering, payroll processing, customer billing

**Semi-Structured Decisions**
- **Semi-structured decisions** have some standard procedures but also require some judgment and analysis.
- Part of the decision can be automated, part requires human judgment
- Require information systems to provide data and analysis
- Examples: credit approval, sales forecasting, equipment maintenance scheduling

**Unstructured Decisions**
- **Unstructured decisions** are novel and non-routine, requiring judgment, intuition, and creativity.
- No standard procedures or clear solutions
- Require human expertise and experience
- Examples: new product development, organizational restructuring, strategic partnerships

### Decision-Making Process

The typical decision-making process involves the following stages:

**Intelligence**
- Identify and define the problem or opportunity
- Gather information about the situation
- Recognize that a decision is needed
- Understand the context and constraints

**Design**
- Develop alternative solutions or courses of action
- Analyze each alternative
- Consider consequences and trade-offs
- Model different scenarios

**Choice**
- Evaluate alternatives against criteria
- Select the best alternative
- Commit to a course of action
- Communicate the decision

**Implementation**
- Execute the chosen solution
- Monitor results and performance
- Make adjustments as needed
- Learn from outcomes

## Managers and Decision Making in the Real World

### Information Requirements

Managers need different types of information depending on their level and function:

**Operational Managers Need:**
- Detailed, current information about day-to-day operations
- Transaction data and status reports
- Real-time alerts about problems
- Performance metrics for their area

**Middle Managers Need:**
- Summary information about departmental performance
- Comparative data across departments
- Trend analysis and forecasts
- Exception reports highlighting problems

**Senior Managers Need:**
- Strategic information about market conditions and competition
- High-level performance summaries
- Long-term trends and forecasts
- Information about external environment

### Challenges in Decision Making

**Information Overload**
- Too much data available can overwhelm decision makers
- Difficulty separating important from unimportant information
- Time constraints limit how much information can be processed

**Incomplete Information**
- Perfect information is rarely available
- Must make decisions with uncertainty
- Risk of making poor decisions based on incomplete data

# Business Intelligence and Business Analytics Support for Decision Making

## What is Business Intelligence?

**Business Intelligence (BI)** describes the tools that enable businesses to collect and organize raw data in order to decipher, analyze, contextualize and derive insights and make informed business decisions. BI examines past and current data to show what has happened and is happening.

BI is often used as an umbrella term for large-scale decision support systems in organizations. Business Intelligence systems employ many technologies and techniques to help business professionals understand how BI and data analysis processes work.

## What is Business Analytics?

**Business Analytics (BA)** uses methods like statistical analysis, data mining, predictive modeling, and machine learning to look at data and turn it into ideas that can be used to plan. Business Analytics uses predictive models to anticipate future trends and outcomes.

## Relationship Between BI and Decision Support Systems

**Business Intelligence and Analytics (BI&A) is fundamentally a subfield of Decision Support Systems (DSS)** that seeks to convert more data into deeper insight. While some claim that DSS has been replaced by BI&A and Big Data, research shows that BI&A has lost its connection to DSS foundational literature.

BI&A systems have demonstrated their potential to enhance decision making; however, the linkage between BI&A and decision support systems has been contested. The purpose of BI&A is to inform and enhance human decision making using computing resources.

## How BI and BA Support Decision Making

### Data-Driven Decision Making
Businesses can sort through vast amounts of data using these vital tools to find valuable insights. This makes the decision-making process more effective and streamlined. Using Business Intelligence as part of your plan can help you make better decisions, spot trends, and run your business more efficiently.

### Proactive vs. Reactive Decisions
Businesses can switch from reacting to decisions to making decisions proactively with BI. It ensures every choice is based on data-driven insights.

### Comprehensive Organizational View
BI provides a foundation for BA, enabling accurate forecasts and proactive strategies. Together, they give a comprehensive view of an organization, supporting informed decisions and strategic planning.

## Key Capabilities of BI and BA Systems

### Data Collection and Integration
BI focuses on the collection, integration, analysis, and presentation of business information to facilitate strategic decisions. BI systems consolidate data from multiple sources into a single, comprehensive view.

### Analysis and Insights
Data analytics involves examining, cleaning, transforming, and modeling data to uncover patterns, draw conclusions, and support decision-making. Business Intelligence and analytics play a crucial role in today's business decision making by inferring unprecedented insights from enterprise data.

### Visualization and Reporting
BI turns data analytics insights into actionable strategies using tools like reports, dashboards, and visualizations, offering a comprehensive view of an organization's performance. OLAP (Online Analytical Processing) enables rapid, intuitive exploration of large data sets by organizing data into multidimensional cubes. This helps business analysts identify trends, relationships and anomalies in data to support effective decision-making.

### Predictive Analytics
Predictive analytics uses historical data, statistical models and machine learning to predict future outcomes.

## Business Value of BI and BA

### Improved Decision Making
BI and BA systems provide the data and analytical capabilities needed to make better-informed decisions. They enable managers to move from intuition-based to data-driven decision making.

### Operational Efficiency
Leveraging BI technologies, businesses can gain valuable insights and improve decision-making. Organizations can improve operational efficiency through better understanding of their business processes and performance.

### Competitive Advantage
Businesses can stay ahead of the competition by using BI and BA to identify market trends, emerging opportunities, and competitive threats.

### Strategic Planning
Data analytics and BI transform overwhelming raw data into clear, actionable insights to guide informed decision-making and strategic planning. BI and BA enable organizations to develop proactive strategies based on predictive insights rather than reactive responses.

## Modern Applications

### Evolving Field
The field of decision support systems (DSS)/business intelligence (BI) is evolving rapidly to become more focused on innovative applications of data streams that were not even captured some time back, much less analyzed in any significant way.

### Consumer and Enterprise Use
Even consumers are using analytics tools directly or indirectly to make decisions on routine activities such as shopping, healthcare, and entertainment.

### Changing Technologies
Changing technologies including mobile devices and cloud computing have created new opportunities for computerized decision support and an increasing need for technology support of business decision making.

## Key Takeaway

Business Intelligence and Business Analytics work together as complementary tools to transform raw data into actionable insights that support informed decision-making at all levels of the organization, enabling businesses to compete effectively in a data-driven global environment.

# Decision Support for Operational, Middle and Senior Management with Business Intelligence

## Overview

Business Intelligence (BI) is often used as the umbrella term for large-scale decision support systems (DSS) in organizations. BI systems support decision-making across all management levels, though the type of support varies based on management level and decision type.

## Decision Support for Operational Management

### Nature of Decisions
Operational managers are generally charged with monitoring the performance of key aspects of the business, ranging from the downtime of machines on a factory floor to daily or hourly sales at franchise food stores to daily traffic at a company's website. Most of the decisions these managers make are fairly structured.

### BI Support for Operational Managers
- **Performance monitoring** - Track key performance indicators and metrics in real-time
- **Structured decision support** - Support routine, repetitive decisions with standard procedures
- **Operational dashboards** - Display current status of business operations
- **Alert systems** - Notify managers of problems requiring immediate attention
- **Transaction data analysis** - Examine details of business transactions

## Decision Support for Middle Management

### Nature of Decisions
Middle managers monitor departmental performance and make tactical decisions that implement organizational strategy. Their decisions are often semi-structured, requiring both standard procedures and judgment.

### BI Support for Middle Management
**Decision Support Systems (DSS) Platform**
Decision-support systems (DSS) are the BI delivery platform for middle managers, with the ability to support semi-structured decision making. These systems enable managers to:

- **Create custom reports** - Generate reports tailored to specific needs
- **Perform sophisticated analytics** - Use advanced analytical tools and models
- **Find patterns in data** - Discover trends and relationships
- **Model alternative scenarios** - Test "what-if" scenarios
- **Test hypotheses** - Validate assumptions with data

### Super Users and Business Analysts
Some managers are "super users" and keen business analysts who want to create their own reports and use more sophisticated analytics and models to find patterns in data, to model alternative business scenarios, or to test specific hypotheses.

## Decision Support for Senior Management

### Nature of Decisions
Senior managers make strategic decisions that shape organizational direction and long-term performance. These decisions are typically unstructured and require judgment, creativity, and external information.

### BI Support for Senior Management
- **Strategic dashboards** - High-level view of organizational performance
- **Executive information systems** - Summary-level data for strategic decisions
- **Trend analysis** - Long-term patterns and forecasts
- **Competitive intelligence** - Market and competitor information
- **Predictive analytics** - Forecasts of future conditions
- **Scenario planning** - Evaluate strategic alternatives

## Key Characteristics of BI for Decision Support

### Comprehensive Data Analysis
BI systems analyze large volumes of data for informing business decisions. They support the management, operations, and planning levels of an organization in making better decisions by assessing the significance of uncertainties and the tradeoffs involved in making one decision over another.

### Accessibility and Democratization
Unlimited user access means that not just analysts and top executives, but everyone from middle managers to front-line employees can engage with data pertinent to their roles. This accessibility is crucial for fostering a data-driven culture where every decision, no matter how small, is informed by real-time data insights.

### Customized Dashboards
Dashboards allow for customized views tailored to different aspects of the businessâ€”from finance and sales to customer service and supply chain management. Each dashboard can be designed to highlight specific metrics that are crucial for particular departments or operational needs.

### Breaking Down Silos
Democratizing data with unlimited users helps break down silos within an organization, facilitating a more integrated approach to business operations.

## BI vs. DSS Relationship

BI and DSS are often conflated, with some experts considering BI a successor to DSS. However, BI systems fundamentally serve as large-scale decision support systems that support decision-making across all organizational levels.

## Organizational Impact

The most important use patterns in decision support are concerned with the type of decision to be supported and the type of manager that makes the decision. Enterprise and smaller functional BI systems exist together in an organization to support different decisions and different decision makers.
